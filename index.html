In pregnancy, a woman’s metabolism is affected, whether or not she is suffering from diabetes melitus. This is because more (additional) production of glucose is required to be made available to the fetus. Hormones estrogen and progesterone, insulinase and increased prolactin have effect in the increased resistance of cells to insulin and/increased speed of insulin breakdown during pregnancy (Smith, 2020, slide 25).

What is Gestational Diabetes?

Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) is a condition in which a hormone made by the placenta prevents the body from using insulin effectively. Glucose builds up in the blood instead of being absorbed by the cells. Unlike type 1 diabetes, gestational diabetes is not caused by a lack of insulin, but by other hormones produced during pregnancy that can make insulin less effective, a condition referred to as insulin resistance. Gestational diabetic symptoms disappear around six weeks following delivery.
courtesy: hopkinsmedicine.

Some of the Risk Factors

* Maternal obesity (BMI >OR= 30kg/m^2).
* Previous unexplained stillbirth or infant having congenital abnormalities.
* Family history of DM.
* History of macrosomic infant (>9lbs).
* History of GDM in a previous pregnancy.
(Smith, 2020, slide 27)


Diagnosis of GDM

Prenatal screening test to identify GDM is routinely performed between 24 and 28 weeks into gestation. It could be done earlier in women with higher risk factors. This screening for GDM can be diagnosed in two different ways.
* The woman drinks 50g of oral glucose solution. This is known as the 50g oral glucose challenge test. After one hour, her blood sample is analysed for glucose; if her blood glucose level is at 7.8mmol/L of greater, that would be an indication for a more comlex test known as -the 3-hr glucose tolerance test.
* A 3-hr glucose tolerance test requiring an initial fasting, then a 1-hr and 2-hrs. Any abnormal result in the 3-hr glucose test is diagnosed for GDM (Keenan-Lindsay, 2020, p. 109).

Complications:

These include: Preeclampsia- this is suspected when high blood pressure and signs of damage to another organ system, most often the liver and kidneys are observed. Usually, it begins after 20 weeks of pregnancy in women whose blood pressure had been normal.

Depression- (the most common form is known as Perinatal depression)- is depression that happens during pregnancy or in the first year after having a baby. Causes of GDM:

What causes gestational diabetes? Gestational diabetes occurs when your body can't make the extra insulin needed during pregnancy. Insulin, a hormone made in your pancreas, helps your body use glucose for energy and helps control your blood glucose levels.
courtesy: Symptoms & Causes of Gestational Diabetes | NIDDK

Gestational diabetes diet

• Plenty of whole fruits and vegetables.
• Moderate amounts of lean proteins and healthy fats.
• Moderate amounts of whole grains, such as bread, cereal, pasta, and rice, plus starchy vegetables, such as corn and peas.
• Fewer foods that have a lot of sugar, such as soft drinks, fruit juices, and pastries.

5 ways to stay healthy with gestational diabetes

Most physicians perform a simple blood test on pregnant women during about 24 to 28 weeks into the pregnancies in other to get a picture of how your body reacts to insulin. If a high blood sugar level is detected, further evaluation test would be recommended involving tests on where your blood sugar is tested every hour for three hours after drinking the sugary solution. Depending on the results, your doctor will determine if you have gestational diabetes. However, should that be the case, below mentioned five simple routines would keep both mother and her baby healthy throughout the gestational period.

1. Create a healthy carb strategy. Swap out refined carbohydrates such as white bread, candy and soda for fibre-filled whole alternatives like whole-grain bread, fruits and vegetables. Spread your intake of carb-containing foods throughout the day to keep your blood sugar steady.
2. Eat small meals throughout the day. Rather than having three large meals per day, opt to eat small meals or low carb snacks every two to four hours.
3. Stay active throughout pregnancy. If you were a regular exerciser before pregnancy, talk to your doctor about sticking with your routine now. Even a short 10-minute walk after every meal can make a huge impact.
4. Keep an eye on your blood sugar levels. Typically, blood sugar levels should be measured after each meal, but your doctor may advise testing before meals and first thing in the morning as well.
5. Share your diagnosis with your primary care doctor. Women with gestational diabetes have a 60 percent chance of developing Type 2 diabetes later in life; so stay in constant contact with your primary care doctor. They may even recommend testing your blood sugar levels every one to three years.
